Overview

The Concept of Foodle

As a society, we've evolved from hunting and gathering food on our own to ordering food through the drive thru at our local eatery. The old time tradition of preparing and eating healthy food has been mostly put aside for the sake of convenience. But at what cost?

What Foodle sets out to achieve is to present its users with the ability to plan what they'd like to eat ahead of time for the week, month, etc. Users will be able to search and select recipes from a database born of collaboration with online sources as well as personal contributions from other users. They will also have the ability to rate their favorite recipes, photograph their food creations, and post those creations for their followers and community to view.

Problem

Ideas Without a Direction

Our client had the idea of creating a new application that would be able to fit in under the broad umbrella of online content storage and organization with a focus on content creation, organization and collaboration. The app needed to be able to leverage the “network effect” to ensure positive growth so collaboration was a big focus. With this in mind I knew that a social sharing application could have a strong foundation to build on. With this as my starting point, I started thinking about what people of all types might have in common.

After researching different ideas I came to the conclusion that food would be an interesting and uniting focal point for this application. It is widely popular with all types of demographics and people love to post and share what they eat when they are eating it. What better way to bring people together than with their obsession with food.

Solution

Finding a Niche Market

The solution I proposed was to explore the idea of food as a unifying concept that could incorporate sharing, content creation and collaboration. These were the biggest deliverables our client requested and following this approach allowed for more creative exploration in a niche area not over-saturated by a multitude of similar applications.

The problem as I saw it was I needed to “Establish an app direction that refined the clients requirements into a deliverable that can stand out in a saturated market.

After reviewing this list again, I saw this as sort of a recipe with each of the requirements as ingredients, with one part organization, a dash or collaboration, a sprinkle of online posting, and the main ingredient. FOOD!

That’s how the idea of Foodle was born.

Strategy

User Survey

Shaping the Idea Through User Input

After researching and gaining an understanding of the features competitors were implementing, I started this step by creating a list of questions that were meant to discover further information about my target audience. I wanted to figure out why users would be interested in using my proposed app and what types of features they would like to see within. The survey was taken by 18 participants in 48 hours.

I structured the questions to learn:

  • A general sense of user technical aptitude.
  • What types of applications are used to serve user needs for Cloud Storage.
  • Understanding of how cloud storage was utilized, and in what situations.
  • How the idea of collaboration was viewed by audiences using applications.
  • How open users were to having sharing and collaboration features in an app featuring food.
  • What was the frequency users cooked for themselves and who they cooked for.
  • Did they share anything online with their social groups they were part of?
  • What types of content do they share?
  • How receptive they were to early feature ideas of the app.

Some key data points were:

  • 44% of users were moderate tech users that relied on a few key apps throughout their day.
  • 72% are familiar and use an online storage/data repository of some form.
  • 92.3% use these services to share files with others and about the same amount of users share links to their files instead of sending the files themselves.
  • 76.9% of people thought the idea for collaboration with others was useful for business applications, but weren’t something they’d regularly use in their personal lives.
  • 66.7% of people cook for themselves and their families regularly with 1-2 days off.
  • 87.5% of people shared what they ate with their social group usually in the form of message posts.
  • The majority 38.9% of users surveyed were between the ages of 25-35.

While the purpose of this survey was to gauge general information from a potential user base, it also was meant to propose feature ideas for my future app. I learned that people generally used collaboration in professional settings and not so much in personal ones. They also tended to share quite a bit online with those in their social groups, whether it be recipes or images of the food they consumed.

User Personas

Deciphering my Target Audience

Of those surveyed, the two biggest age groups were ages 25-35 (38.9%) and 18-25 (22.2%). Of those two demographics, two main types of people were more readily apparent; students and professionals.

Filtering down the data into these two different personas gave me some insight on what types of features I should be considering.

User Stories

Seeking Out Competition

After reviewing the results of the user survey I performed earlier as well as the new personas, I listed out my feature ideas and user tasks that could be used in the app. From my point of view of each persona, I also envisioned the subject matter on each screen that the users would be interacting with, and put together tasks based on the data collected and grouped them not only by new or returning users, but priority as well.

I created 24 user stories sorted by priority to help me choose what tasks were more important to include in the app MVP.

Some of these tasks include:

As a new user...

  • I want to be able to log in with a username and password so that I can return to the app at any time and view my personal configurations and settings.
  • I'd like to be able to use the app with basic functionality as a guest user without signing in so that I can at least try out without providing personal information.
  • I want to be able to see a visual onboarding process so that I can understand how to work within the app.

As a returning user...

  • I want to be able to search for something on any screen I interact with so I can find what I'm looking for easily.
  • I want to be able to speak to Siri or comparable mobile AI to ask questions, get recipes, look up ingredients, etc so that if my hands are busy, I can still use the app.
  • To have a user participate in contests, or streaks where I collect badges, coupons for completion so that I can have some positive reinforcement that I'm making good food decisions.

Doing this step in the process allowed for more creativity in what features I wanted to explore. Assigning priority also provided a way to differentiate a general nice to have feature, a standard feature, and a critical need feature.

User Flows

Visualizing User Task Paths

Considering the amount of tasks that were initially considered for the app, after assigning priority in the previous exercise I began the work of planning how to go about completing each task by mapping out user flows. I also chose to develop the app MVP as an iOS application instead of a web application as I wanted to leverage some of the hardware of the mobile device.

Here are the 13 user flows that I chose to work with in.

  • Log in with a username and password.
  • Use the app with basic functionality as a guest user.
  • See a visual on boarding process.
  • Access and view my settings at all times.
  • Link my social media accounts to the app.
  • See my feed showing different posts, recipes of the day.
  • Use the mobile device camera.
  • Tag my photographs.
  • Select individual recipes based on different criteria.
  • Share the recipes I find via text, tweet, or email.
  • View a recipe, and scale the ingredients up or down.
  • Dump all ingredients to a list.
  • Create a meal planning calendar.

Getting my user flows down on paper gave me a good start as to how many steps users would take to get to the end of the task. It also allowed me to get to visualize how those steps would need to be connected to other steps.

Sketches / Wireframes

Laying out the Bones

Using the information provided by the sitemap, I started to work out how I wanted information to be shown on each screen of the app. On paper I drew what I believed would be a rough representation of the screens that I would show in each feature. At the stage, I wasn’t worried about content or detail and just put the thoughts to paper. Once I was satisfied with the arrangement of the elements on each app screen, I proceeded to create low fidelity wireframes in Figma and prepared the screens for my first usability test.

Usability Test 01

Testing Wireframe User Flows

For my first round of testing I chose testers that represented the demographics that my personas were built on and let them have a go at the prototype. These initial tests were done with two in a personal setting and one remotely. While I worked well with the two that were done in the same room, the one done remotely was a bit challenging since I wasn’t able to read the body language of test subject like the other two.

Working with the test subjects in person, I was able to listen to them as they worked through the scenarios that I provided them. They all had familiar working with mobile apps before and we didn’t have a moment where they were stumped, however they did provide some valuable insight on what I could improve upon in later testing, since this was only showing plain wireframes.

One thing I learned in these tests was how privacy was a concern amongst these users. I learned that users wanted to try out the app prior to providing their personal info, just in case they didn't want to commit fully to the app. Adding another place to sign in after bypassing the welcome / login screens was what I thinking to solve this problem.

Adding confirmation screens and user feedback screens were also requested as when users where interacting with the wireframe prototype, they were expecting more details to be shown on screen other than the basics of each screen.

Preference Testing

Testing Design Concepts

After establishing a concise direction for the art style of the MVP, there were a few times where I went back and forth about including a specific style of an element. Adding Preference Testing to this project allowed for testers to remark whether they liked one different style or another. I found this quite useful as I was looking for more immediate snap reactions other than long drawn out reasons why they liked one or another. Testing was done via link provided through Slack and other messaging apps, and subjects were friends and other peers from Bloc. While the test was active, I had a total of 20 participants that gave their opinions.

Test 01

Which Tab Bar Design Do You Prefer?

Since Foodle was meant to provide users a way to photograph their content to share with other users, I wanted to have a dedicated camera button for users to easily tap to access this feature. My first attempt to address this was to have it as an icon on the Tab Bar with its name below. While it conveyed the message, another approach I wanted to go with was having a circular button in the center of the bar. This was similar to the shutter button on iOS and I believed users would relate to this one more. I was correct in that assumption, however, what I found was the protruding arch at the top of the button was covering content, and I found that problematic when scrolling through content. I took the idea of B and confined it to the height of the Tab Bar. The final solution looked much cleaner and still was easy to understand.

Usability Test 02

Testing Mockup MVP

With the second round of testing, I went a different approach and did these tests all remotely. I received some valuable feedback that allowed my prototypes to be more expressive in the future, along with the benefit for getting better feedback as well. However, using this method caused my time spent working with my prototype to balloon more than expected. Instead of testing linear user flows like before, I prototyped all the screens together in a comprehensive prototype. While time consuming to put together, I learned this method would allow complete immersion of the user in the app, and other than a fully functional HTML prototype, this was the next best thing.

In this prototype, I categorized two sets of screens between the user signing in or not.. This took the testers to different screens and even though the testing was done over cellular, they were impressed with the level of thought and detail that was put into the prototypes. The feedback given by the users was more visual than interaction based, and I made sure that most of it was implemented it in the final version of Foodle's MVP.

Conclusion

My Thoughts on the Foodle Project

What Worked?

I think the idea to solve the client requirements was done fairly adequately. I purposely didn’t want to go to another Dropbox clone, and I believe the attempt to take the idea I came up with and work it to meet the client feature requirement worked out fairly well. I’m happy with the feedback that I’ve received from testers regarding the visuals of this project, and glad they add to the final polished look of the MVP. The idea of this app was fairly solid and I received a good bit of feedback on how it could be used by people in the future if it was expanded upon and created.

What Didn't

I think in the current technological era there are so many things grabbing for your attention, people take for granted on how those things actually came into existence. Not many people find it fascinating to see the early steps of how something came to be, they just want to see the finished product. Feedback is important and necessary to keep a project progressing, and if the feedback is more towards the end result instead of the step that you’re at, the progression stagnates a bit. Asking questions and following up is a key factor I learned to help keep pushing progress forward.

What Surprised You?

The level of detail that I went through going into the usability testing. Not only did I have to coordinate with others, but I provided a great amount of detail about each encounter. Since this has been done before, I did the same amount of note taking as done previously, but it’s hard to see how a good majority of it was beneficial as it wasn’t leveraged in the revisions. Maybe with more testing in the future, my approach will become more concise instead of trying to distill the whole scene onto paper.

Bottom Line Takeaway

Gauging the time needed to do a thorough project is something that is hard to visualize in the beginning. Having a great idea and giving yourself time to do the research beforehand will save you a lot of time in the future.
